Zach is not a prodigy coder. He is not a marketing genius. He is not a management savant. What he is, is dangerous at all three. And the combination is what built a $30M/year company before he turned 20.

The approach is the Pareto principle applied to skill acquisition: learn the 20% of each skill that gets you 80% of the results, then stack multiple skills together. Zach learned enough coding to understand how to lead a technical team and evaluate developers. He learned enough marketing to run influencer campaigns and paid ads. He learned enough management to hire people smarter than him in each domain.

The mistake most aspiring founders make is thinking they need to be a 900 IQ genius to enter a space. Zach explicitly calls this out as a misconception he held early on. The reality is simpler: give any skill 6 months of full focus and you will be good enough to be dangerous. That is all you need at one skill — then you stack two or three others.

This is especially powerful in the age of AI tools that lower the technical bar for building products. The differentiator is no longer deep expertise in one domain. It is the ability to combine competence across building, selling, and leading into a package that no specialist can replicate.
